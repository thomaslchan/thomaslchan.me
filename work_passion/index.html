Finding passion in work


My interest in biology started young. I asked about a million questions about how life works that my parents could never answer. Ironically, my formal introduction in 7th grade provided little help as well. The teacher deducted points for my first quiz - apparently plants produce "food" and not sugar. Thankfully, I had the opportunity to take honors biology in 9th grade with a more competant teacher who consistently answered all of my questions. (Shoutout to Deanna Hoffamn for inspiring me to pursue a career in biology.)

My love for computer science, on the other hand, was pure chance. My friend suggested I take Advanced Placement Computer Science A with her for our senior year elective. Her parents were software engineers and recommended it. At this point, I had no idea what computer science entailed. I thought I would hack computers and build hardware. Unlike me, most people in the class had previous experience or external access to professionals. Needless to say, I felt like an underdog. But I eventually began to thrive and found happiness in the work I was doing.

These formative experiences in high school guided my decision to double major in biology and computer science upon matriculating at Tufts University. I didn't know exactly what careers were tailored for the two fields, but I knew they made me happy and that I wanted to continue studying them. I did exactly that for the next three and a half years.

Studying biology and computer science at the university level introduced new obstacles. Though there were times when the workload or material was more difficult than in high school, I was always able to manage myself. The real distinction from my previous experience was due to the nature of the work.

The decision to study biology and computer science was purely due to passion. It's because I feel tied to the work emotionally; I am invested in the process and the outcomes to the point where it feels effortless. But I didn't always feel this way when doing work for class.

High school biology was a time to ask unlimited questions. You weren't confined to the scope of the course. This seemingly changed in college. Furthermore, the degrees required courses that weren't necessarily of interest, while my high school curriculum focused more on concepts that interested me. (Though, I did love general genetics and molecular biology at Tufts.)

It had gotten to the point where I would doubt my interest the subjects. But I realized my disatisfaction was because I viewed the work as a chore. I remembered how much fun I in the classes I loved, and how those feelings now felt so foreign in classes I was forced to take. But this didn't have to be the case.

I found ways to reignite the feelings I had since lost. I focused on reframing the work. I started viewing debugging code as a puzzle I need to solve or finding relevance to topics that interest me. Recognizing value in my assignments and future applications of gained skills also helped my enthusiasm.

I realized these skills could be applied to my research. There are always parts of the process that I look forward too, that make me want to stay in the lab until late at night. But there are also parts that seem laborious. These newly aquired techniques can definitely extend to my future work.

I'm thankful for having jumped into my passions without hesitance. My passion for the two disciplines has evolved. I still love them just as much, but I now love them more fully. I am able to love more than just the "good" parts. I can enjoy the parts I didn't think I could love before.
