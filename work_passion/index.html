Finding passion in work


My passion for biology started young. I asked a million questions about how life worked that my parents could never answer. I finally had a formal introduction in 7th grade, but my experience was less than ideal. The teacher deducted points for my first quiz - apparently plants produce "food" and not sugar. Thankfully, I had the opportunity to take honors biology in 9th grade with Deanna Hoffman, who consistently answered all of my questions.

My love for computer science, on the other hand, was pure chance. My friend suggested I take AP Computer Science A with her for our senior year elective. Her parents were software engineers and recommended it. At this point, I had no idea what computer science was. I thought I would hack computers and build hardware. Most people in the class had previous experience or expert resources externally. I felt like an underdog, but I began to thrive and found happiness in the work I was doing.

These formative experiences in high school guided my decision to double major in biology and computer science upon matriculating at Tufts University. I didn't know exactly what career I was aiming for with the two fields, but I knew they made me happy and that I wanted to continue studying them. I did exactly that for the next three and a half years.

Studying biology and computer science at the university level was different at the high school level. Though there were times when the workload or material was more difficult, I was always able to manage myself. The real distinction from my previous experience was due to the nature of the work.

I decided to study biology and computer science was purely due to passion. It's because I feel tied to the work emotionally; I am invested in the process and the outcomes to the point where it feels effortless. But I didn't always feel this way when doing work for class. Some assignments just didn't pique my interest, which is strange considering I always felt strong interest in the concepts discussed in class.

It had gotten to the point where I forgot why I liked the subjects. But I realized I just wasn't deriviing enjoyment from the work I was doing; it felt like a chore. I remembered how much fun I had making Space Invaders in AP CS and doing problem sets in AP Biology. But through that discovery, I found a way to get through it and reignite my love for CS and biology. I needed to reframe my work, treat the problems like games or find some relevance to a topic I find important.
