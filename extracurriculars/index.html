Value in extracurriculars


As my time at Tufts is coming to a close, I find myself reflecting more on the experiences and forms of growth that have occurred since I first step foot on campus. I've realized how much I've learned — not only in class, but outside of the classroom. Some of my favorite college memories were made through my extracurricular activities.

Before I arrived at Tufts, I reached out to the news editor of the Tufts Daily. I was heavily involved in my high school publication, and wanted to continue in college. I worked as a copy editor for a year and a web editor for another, but I then left to pursue other interests. I felt obligated to continue for the sake of demonstrating committment, but I also wasn't finding the same fulfillment as I did with Wayland Student Press Network. Although this was a hard decision, it was an important step in recognizing how I can box myself in.

This pressure I felt was not an isolated case. I was also part of Tufts University Social Collective (TUSC), resposible for organizing social events for the student body. I had a wonderful time and continued with it through my sophomore year. But with junior year approaching, I needed to better balance my schedule and not spread myself so thin. Leaving TUSC, only compounded my anxiety. I was worried about showing committment and leaving two clubs in a year would be scar on my record.

Other issues that came up were the need for extracurriculars that were relevant to my professional life. I needed more experience and wanted to show work I did outside of the classroom. I joined major-related organizations like Out in Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics (oSTEM) or JumboCode. oSTEM is a pre-professional organization that helped me network with other queer individuals, and JumboCode allowed me to make software for non-governmental organizations free-of-charge!

I am incredibly greatful for my time at these organizations. I learned so, so much — things that I could never learn in a classroom setting. And I've made connections with people that transcended club settings. I still keep in touch with those who've graduated and those still in clubs I have since left. But I've also learned a lot from my troubles with leaving these clubs and exploring other extracurriculars.

I realized how important is to prioritize my own happiness. Worrying about what appears on a resume is a valid concern, but it shouldn't compromise your happiness or wellbeing. Even more, boxing yourself into extracurriculars for the sake of committment can be exhausting, and speading yourself too thin can actually be more unproductive.

Secondly, you can show committment through continued use of skill even if you're developing these skills through different mean. (It's like having new internships each summer.) Although I left TUSC, I still organized events through Tufts Computer Science Exchange (CSX). I joined Tufts CSX my sophomore year, eventually becoming president and now shifting to a more advisory role.

Finally, the most important thing I've learned is the value of exploring extracurriculars that are not necessarily linked to your major. I enjoyed developing for the Tufts Daily and JumboCode, but I also learned so much through the non-academic work I did through my other activities. I was a leader of eight incoming students for First-Year Orientation CommUnity Service (FOCUS). The program allowed me to mentor first-years through LGBTQ+ and racial equity-based community service. A form of work that is so rewarding in ways different from academic work.

Some cool things I've done through my activies include:
— Organizing a $200,000+ carnival for the entire student body and communities surrounding Tufts
— Writing and defending a $13,000 club budget to the Tufts Community Union treasurer
— Participating in a 12-week training program and attending a follow-up conference in Detroit, MI
— Raise more than $20,000 in sponsorship funds for Polyhack, the annual hackathon, which I coordinated in 2018
— Working in a large team to create an application for STOMP, an on-campus organization that promotes engineering to K-12 students
— Become a 4-year FOCUSer (a rare achievement where one is a participant of FOCUS as a first-year and staff member for the next three years) — Being part of the Daily (Tufts is the smallest college in the U.S. with a daily print paper.)
