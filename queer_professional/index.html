Being a queer professional


Why am I sharing this on my professional website? I would have asked myself the same thing a few years ago.

I came out as gay a week before college. Things have changed (for the better) since then. I have become more comfortable with myself â€” my queer identity in particular. To clarify, queer is an umbrella term reclaimed by activists and academics to refer to any LGBTQ identity.

During my sophomore year, I attended Out for Undergrad Tech Conference (O4U Tech), a pre-professional conference for high-achieving LGBTQ+ students interested in technology. Here, I learned about the value of being out in the workplace.

The fight for gender and sexual minorities has made great strides in past years, but there is still a heavy stigma surrounding these identities. It is a struggle to be open about this in the workplace; expectations being cisgender or having heterosexual relationships is the norm, and some states do not offer legal protections from discrimination against LGBTQ+ employees.

I choose to disclose my name and pronoun preference because visibility in the workplace is important to me. Sure, including these facts may be off-putting to some employers, but I would not be content working for an unsupportive company anyway. I should feel safe in the workplace as should any of my cisgender, heterosexual counterparts. Furthermore, making myself visible can hopefully lead to other queer folk being more comfortable in the workplace.

There are compromises I still make as a queer individual. For exampe, I still use my legal name for professional purposes. This is because many people hold inherent biases and will likely hold less regard for the work of someone with a non-traditional name. Is this forsaking my queer identity or my values of visibility? I don't think so. I'm using my legal name in a situation where nuance cannot be delivered (ie. through the author line of a PDF) and using my preferred name otherwise (ie. in face-to-face interaction).

So yes, maybe it is controversial that I'm sharing this information. And the question may still stand: why am I sharing this? But to that I ask, why is sharing this considered controversial?
