Being a queer professional


I feel hesitant posting this, but I know it's the right thing to do. Why am I sharing this on my professional website? I would have asked myself the same thing a few years ago.

I came out as gay a week before college, and realized I was transgender two and a half years later. Since then, I have become more comfortable with my queer identity. (Queer is an umbrella term reclaimed by activists and academics to refer to any LGBTQ+ identity.)

During my sophomore year, I attended the Out for Undergrad Tech Conference (O4U Tech), a pre-professional conference for high-achieving LGBTQ+ students interested in technology. Here, I learned about the value of being out in the workplace.

Despite advances in past years, acceptance of gender and sexual minorities is still not realized. Acknowledging queer identities in the workplace is still considered nonprofessional behavior, and some states do not offer legal protections from discrimination against LGBTQ+ employees.

Before, I would disclose it based on my perceptions of the company. I would ask myself, "is this company or industry openly accepting of queer individuals?" If I did choose to disclose it, I would only acknowledge it through professional contexts. For example, on my resume, I would include the O4U Tech conference or Out in Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics (oSTEM) on my resume.

I now disclose my preferred name and pronoun preference in hope to make queerness more visible in the workplace. Sure, including these facts may be off-putting to some employers, but I would not be content working for an unsupportive institution anyway. I should feel as safe in the workplace as any of my cisgender, heterosexual counterparts.

There are nuances to "outness," especially in the workplace. While I feel comfortable sharing this information, I do not expect other queer people to do the same. Coming out is a continual process; I'm constantly meeting new people and have to decide how to be out to them and to what extent. It always depends on my own comfort and prioritizing safety.

For example, I still use my legal name for professional purposes. This is because many people hold less regard for people with non-traditional names, and in turn, may have biases toward their work. Is this forsaking my values of visibility? I don't think so. I view it more as code-switching: I use my legal name in contexts where nuance cannot be delivered (eg. through the author line of an article), and I use my preferred name otherwise (eg. in face-to-face interaction).

Maybe it is controversial that I'm sharing this information, but this shouldn't be the case. My identity is not deviant or inappropriate, not even in a workplace environment. And, I want to make that clear. If you feel the need to ask me why I am sharing this, I'd ask in return why you feel the need to ask me that question in the first place.
