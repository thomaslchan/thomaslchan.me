Discovering research as a career


I find it funny that people discuss degrees in terms of employability. I definitely acknowledged the resourcefulness of having a degree in both biology and computer science, but economic gain was far from my primary motive in choosing the two fields. Nevertheless, I'd be rich (ironically) if I got a penny every time somebody told me how employable my degrees are.

Despite these comments, I didn't know what career path to follow given my two degrees. I wanted to do something that involved biology and computer science. I was studying them in separate spheres during the academic year and wanted to experience them in an interdisciplinary context for the summer. I also knew that internships are a pathway to a full-time career — I didn't want to set myself onto a path involving only one field. (I can't live without both of them.)

So, for my sophomore year, I applied to National Science Foundation programs, specifically research experiences for undergraduates (REUs). One of the programs I applied to was at the Boyce Thompson Institute (BTI), a Cornell-affiliated lab specializing in plant research. I had never had formal research experience, so this was a great opportunity. Additionally, I am very interested in high-impact, mission-driven work. The lab I chose focuses on improving crop yields and works with institutions in developing nations.

Needless to say, I had an amazing time at BTI and fell in love with Ithaca. But I felt like I needed to try out pure software engineering (SWE). I applied to both REUs and SWE roles the next summer and managed to get an interview with Google. I went to final round interviews, but did not get an offer. I'm sharing this because it's important to acknowledge failure (especially in the sciences). But I learned a lot from the process and am thankful for having gone through it.

Looking back, I'm glad I was rejected from Google — otherwise, I would have taken the offer and missed my experiences at Harvard Medical School (HMS). But also, isn't hindsight 20/20? Talking to my peers, including friends who have interned at Google, I'm confident in my choice to not enter SWE. Without going into too much detail, the nature of research seems to be a better fit for me.

I applied to REUs because I knew it was the right path. The doubt I had in my mind was because my peers were pursuing the traditional SWE route, and I thought, "What if?" I don't blame myself for that because it's a valid consideration. I am thankful for my time at HMS independent of SWE; I learned so much and befriended many intentional, intelligent individuals.

I see my journey like answering a multiple-choice question. I could find the answer by process of elimination (ie. trying out SWE and deciding I don't like it), or I can be so confident in my answer that I don't need to consider the other options (ie. considering my time at BTI and HMS as life-changing). And maybe to the person reading this, it seems like I'm trusting in blind faith instead of trying other options. But to me, it felt intrinsically right doing biology and computer science research.
